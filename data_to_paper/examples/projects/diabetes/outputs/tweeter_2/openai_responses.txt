Individuals with diabetes were more likely to report poorer physical health compared to those without diabetes. This underscores the significant implications of diabetes on overall well-being. Importantly, our study investigated the mediating role of BMI in the relationship between diabetes and physical health. We found that BMI partially mediates this association, with higher BMI levels increasing the risk of poor physical health among individuals with diabetes (Eitner et al., 2020). These findings align with the existing body of evidence that highlights the link between obesity and adverse health outcomes in individuals with diabetes. Obesity has been associated with increased risks of cardiovascular diseases, insulin resistance, and complications related to diabetes (Heiat et al., 2002; Grundy et al., 2004). Addressing BMI-related factors should be a priority in diabetes management to improve physical health outcomes.\n\nAnother noteworthy aspect of our study is the identification of age as a significant moderator of the relationship between diabetes and physical health. We found that older individuals with diabetes experience a stronger negative impact on physical health compared to their younger counterparts. Age-related factors, such as reduced physiological resilience and increased comorbidities, may contribute to this phenomenon. Consistent with prior research, our result demonstrates the association between older age and poor health outcomes in individuals with diabetes (Walker et al., 2016; Eitner et al., 2020). Aging is known to exacerbate the complications and management challenges associated with diabetes, including increased risks of cardiovascular complications and functional limitations (Kirkman et al., 2012). Recognizing age as a critical factor is important in tailoring interventions and care strategies to address the unique needs of older individuals with diabetes.\n\nIt is important to acknowledge the limitations of our study, as they may impact the generalizability and validity of the findings. First, our study relied on self-report surveys, which may introduce recall bias and misclassification of diabetes and health indicators. The use of objective measures and additional validation techniques would enhance the accuracy of data collection. Second, the cross-sectional design of our study limits our ability to establish causality. Longitudinal studies would provide further insights into the temporal relationships between diabetes, BMI, age, and physical health. Additionally, our analysis focused on a single year (2015) and may not capture temporal changes in these relationships.\n\nDespite these limitations, our findings have significant implications for diabetes management and public health strategies. Addressing BMI and age-related factors should be integral components of diabetes management programs aimed at improving overall health outcomes. Tailored interventions targeted at weight management, physical activity promotion, and age-specific care protocols may help mitigate the adverse effects of higher BMI levels and aging on physical health. Furthermore, our results highlight the need for a comprehensive and individualized approach to diabetes care, considering not only biomedical factors but also addressing psychosocial and environmental determinants of health.\n\nIn conclusion, our study contributes to the existing literature by uncovering the mediating role of BMI and the moderating effect of age in the association between diabetes and physical health. Higher BMI levels were found to increase the risk of poor physical health among individuals with diabetes, with older individuals experiencing a more pronounced negative impact. These findings underscore the importance of addressing BMI and age-related factors in diabetes management to improve overall health outcomes. Moving forward, future research should explore the effectiveness of targeted interventions aimed at managing BMI and addressing the unique health needs of older individuals with diabetes. Ultimately, such efforts will be instrumental in improving the health outcomes and quality of life for individuals living with diabetes."
